SECRET #3

"How To Avoid Scams"

Criminals steal more money from American seniors than from any other group — over three billion dollars a year. Not because seniors are foolish, but because criminals write scripts designed for polite, trusting people. This lesson gives you the rules that make those scripts useless.

The golden rule

Nobody legitimate will ever rush you. Urgency is the fingerprint of every scam. "Act now." "Don't hang up." "Don't tell your family." The moment you feel pressure, you are talking to a criminal. Real banks, real government, and real family can always wait an hour.

Rule 1 — Hang up and call back

If "your bank," "Medicare," or "the IRS" calls you — hang up. Then call the number printed on the back of your card or on your statement. If the call was real, they'll say so. It never is. This one habit defeats nearly every phone scam ever invented, and no honest caller will ever mind.

Rule 2 — Gift cards are always a scam

No government agency, utility company, tech company, or grandchild's lawyer has ever legitimately asked to be paid in gift cards. Anyone who says "go to the store and buy gift cards" is a criminal — 100% of the time, no exceptions in history.

Rule 3 — The family code word

The cruelest scam is a call or voice message that sounds exactly like your grandchild: "Grandma, I'm in trouble, please don't tell Mom." Computers can now copy anyone's voice from a video clip. The defense is simple and old-fashioned: agree on a family code word this week. Any real emergency call from family must include it. No code word — hang up and call them back on their normal number.

Rule 4 — Never click the link

Texts and emails saying "your package is delayed," "your account is locked," or "unusual activity detected" all want the same thing: your tap. Don't click links in messages you didn't ask for. If you're worried about your account, open the app or type the website yourself. A real problem will be waiting there; a fake one won't exist.

Rule 5 — The pop-up that screams "virus"

If your computer suddenly shows a flashing warning with a phone number to call — that is not Microsoft, not Apple, and not a virus. It's an ad written by criminals. Never call the number. Close the browser (or hold the power button and restart). Nothing is wrong with your machine.

Rule 6 — Nobody good asks for secrecy or remote control

Two final tells: anyone who says "don't tell your family" and anyone who asks to "connect to your computer to fix it." Both requests come only from people trying to rob you.

If it already happened

No shame — these criminals fool lawyers and professors too, and embarrassment is their best protection. Act fast: call your bank first, then report it at reportfraud.ftc.gov or call your local police non-emergency line. The faster you call, the more money gets recovered.
