SECRET #4

"How To Use Zoom"

Zoom is a free video telephone. It puts your grandchildren's faces in your living room, your doctor in your kitchen, and your church service anywhere you are. And here is the part nobody tells seniors: joining a Zoom call is easier than programming a VCR ever was.

What you need

Any phone, tablet, or computer made in the last ten years — the camera and microphone are already built in. One-time setup: install the free Zoom app from the App Store (iPhone/iPad) or Play Store (Android). On a computer, Zoom installs itself the first time you join a meeting. You do not need an account to join someone else's call.

Joining a call — the whole recipe

Someone who wants to see you will send a link by text or email. It looks like zoom.us/j/123456789.

Step 1 — Tap the link.
Step 2 — Tap "Open" or "Join Meeting" when asked.
Step 3 — When it asks about audio, tap "Wifi audio" or "Join with Computer Audio."
That's it. You're on the call. Three taps.

The two buttons that matter

Tap the middle of the screen once and buttons appear along the bottom. You only need two:

Mute (microphone icon) — red slash means they can't hear you. If everyone says "we can't hear you," tap it once.
Video (camera icon) — red slash means they can't see you. Tap to turn your face on or off.

Everything else on that bar is optional. Two buttons — that is the entire skill.

Look and sound your best

• Sit facing a window — light in front of you, never behind you.
• Prop the phone up against some books rather than holding it; steady picture, no tired arm.
• The camera is the little dot at the top of the phone — glance at it when you talk and you'll be "making eye contact."

The no-internet backup

Every Zoom invitation includes phone numbers under "One tap mobile" or "Dial in." Call the number from any telephone, punch in the meeting ID when asked, and you join by voice alone — no internet, no app, no camera. If technology ever fails you, the plain old telephone still gets you in the room.

Practice assignment

This week, ask one family member to send you a test Zoom link for a five-minute call. Tell them it's homework. Nobody has ever refused a grandparent asking for a video call — and after one successful test, you'll never hesitate to tap a Zoom link again.
