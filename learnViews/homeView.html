"Mangé Lakay" Project

“...if Haiti could be kept free from revolutions, she might easily become, in proportion to her territory and population, the richest country in the world...” — Frederick Douglass (First American Minister to Haiti)

What do Singapore, China, South Korea, and Botswana have in common?

They all went through a major transformation — starting extremely poor and becoming wealthy nations.

China is the clearest example. Adam Smith (the “father” of capitalism) described 18th-century China like this:

“The poverty of the lower ranks of people in China far surpasses that of the most beggarly nations in Europe…”

And this:

“Marriage is encouraged in China, not by the profitableness of children, but by the liberty of destroying them…”

That was China then. This is China now.

Today China is the second wealthiest nation in the world. Here is what changed:

China: $76 GDP per capita (1961) → $12,597 (2023)
Singapore: $425 (1961) → $84,734+ (2023)
South Korea: $158 (1961) → $35,563+ (2023)
Botswana: $63 (1961) → $7,249+ (2023)

Did they do a good job? Do you want to know how?

They adopted capitalism and allocated capital properly.

Why “Mangé Lakay” — and why now

With Mangé Lakay, we are applying those same principles to create value for the people of Kotfè, Haiti, and our shareholders.

Right now Kotfè’s economy is in dire condition — it has regressed since the 1970s.

Haiti: $68 GDP per capita (1961) → $1,693 (2023)
  • Botswana started $5 poorer than Haiti — now ~4× richer.
  • Singapore was ~6× richer than Haiti in 1961 — now ~50× richer.

Our city is moving backward. The canal my grandfather helped build in the 1950s is still the only water system for farmers — and it is poorly maintained.

Why Kotfè?

  • Most citizens spend 90% of income on food
  • Many eat only 4 days per week on average
  • Many walk miles daily to fetch water
  • Lower security risk than the capital
  • It is my hometown — my full family lineage is here
  • Haiti is a free-market nation with enormous untapped opportunity

What we are building toward

  • $20B+ of value for Kotfè
  • $100B+ indirect value for Haiti
  • 9,000% return for shareholders over our horizon

Returns are built over a 40-year time horizon — not overnight. Each dollar invested now is targeted to become $9,000 over that horizon.

Our model: start with profitable agriculture, amass capital, and allocate into capital-intensive industries over time.

40-year roadmap

Decade 1 — Food

  • 150,000 eggs/day (~$8M annual revenue)
  • Corn surplus to feed 100,000 people for a year
  • Scale to 200,000 people/day (~$100M revenue)
  • Vetiver — biofuel and erosion control

Decade 2 — Development

Buildings, schools, churches, canals, farming consulting, and value-added food products.

Decade 3 — Transportation

Taxis, tap-taps, buses, tourist transport, and distribution networks.

Decade 4 — Infrastructure

Roads, airports, ports, canals, water systems, and energy.

What we have done — and why we exist

We started selling boiled eggs — a popular breakfast in Haiti. With egg cost and labor, we earned a 30% profit margin.

Then we hit a wall: we ran out of eggs to buy.

At Walmart I can buy 40+ cartons of 60 eggs in one day. In Kotfè (50,000+ people), the market could not supply more than 100 eggs.

That scarcity is why Mangé Lakay exists: we must supply the market ourselves.

What we have today

Land

  • 5 centièmes under management (rented through October 2033)
  • ~10 centièmes expected via inheritance (not yet in the fund)

At 2 m² per chicken, each centième supports ~64 chickens.

Livestock (focus through 2034)

  • 45 hens — daily egg production, meat after ~2 years
  • 5 roosters — breeding stock

Other operating assets

  • Charge-your-electronics — covers chicken feed costs
  • Mobile top-up — ~40% return on capital

Risks (full transparency)

This investment is speculative. Haiti is not known as a safe vehicle for capital allocation.

Recommended allocation: 90% conservative assets / 10% Mangé Lakay. That is my personal approach.

We publish quarterly reports. We cannot guarantee outcomes — only honest reporting.

Why Kotfè still wins

  • Underserved market — room to profit where supply is scarce
  • Growth runway — ~7% GDP growth in developing nations vs ~2.5% in the US
  • Competitive labor — ~$0.80/hour average
  • US trade advantages — HOPE/HELP duty-free agricultural exports

How much things cost

Figures in HTG and USD — subject to change.

  • 1 chicken — $12 USD
  • 1 centième — $300 USD
  • 1 labourer/day — $6 USD
  • Building/chicken — $5.57–$10.14 USD
Hen (2-year lifetime): ~$71–$91 total cost
Rooster (12 weeks): ~$25–$35 total cost

How much we can earn

Hens: ~500 days of eggs + meat = $120 lifetime revenue

Roosters: one-time meat sale ≈ $10 lifetime

Will we be profitable?

Hens: $120 − $71.52 ≈ +$48.48 profit per bird

Roosters: ~−$3.37 to −$15.37 (loss mitigated by breeding)

Blended profit per bird: ~$16.55–$22.55

First $100K deployment

  • $2,900 — yearly operating costs
  • $97,000 — fixed costs (land, housing, stock)

Start with ~1,000 birds, scale to ~2,625 in year one.

Chicken farm target

150,000 laying hens (~300,000 total birds).

At full scale: ~$4.97M annual profit. Feed Kotfè’s 50,000 residents 3 eggs/day.

Phase 1 market: ~2M people across Sud-Est, Sud, Nippes, Grand’Anse.
